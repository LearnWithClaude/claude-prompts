Writing
Make Claude write in your voice, not its own
4 prompts
01
Write anything in your exact voice
Feed Claude 3 examples of your writing and it mirrors your style perfectly from then on.
▾
I want you to write like me. Here are 3 examples of my writing style: Example 1: [paste something you wrote] Example 2: [paste something else you wrote] Example 3: [paste a third piece] Based on these examples, identify my voice: sentence length, tone, how formal I am, what phrases I use. Then write [what you need written] in that exact voice. Do not make it sound like AI. Make it sound like me.
voice matchwriting
02
Turn bullet points into polished prose
Paste rough notes and get a structured, readable document back in seconds.
▾
Turn these rough notes into a polished, well-structured [document type — e.g. "report" / "email" / "article" / "proposal"]. My notes: [paste your bullet points or rough draft] Requirements: - Tone: [professional / conversational / authoritative] - Length: [short and punchy / comprehensive / one page] - Audience: [who will read this] Keep all my ideas. Just make them flow properly.
writingediting
03
Critique any piece of writing ruthlessly
Get the honest feedback most people are too polite to give you.
▾
You are a sharp, honest editor. Do not be polite — be useful. Read this piece and give me: 1. The 3 biggest structural problems 2. The sentences that lose the reader 3. Where I'm being vague when I should be specific 4. One thing that's actually working well 5. A rewritten version of the weakest paragraph Here's my writing: [paste your writing]
editingfeedback
04
Rewrite anything shorter and sharper
Cut the fat without losing the point. Works on emails, reports, bios, anything.
▾
Rewrite the following to be [50% shorter / under 100 words / under 3 sentences]. Rules: - Keep every important idea - Cut filler, repetition, and hedging words - Don't change the meaning or tone - If you have to choose what to cut, tell me what you removed and why Here's the text: [paste your text]

Research
Turn Claude into your personal research assistant
4 prompts
05
Deep-dive any topic in 5 minutes
Get a proper briefing on anything — not a Wikipedia summary, a structured breakdown you can act on.
▾
Give me a comprehensive briefing on [topic]. Structure it like this: 1. What it is (plain English, 2 sentences) 2. Why it matters right now 3. The 3 things most people get wrong about it 4. Key terms I need to know 5. The most important thing someone should do with this knowledge I'm a [beginner / intermediate / professional] in this area. Keep the language [simple / technical / balanced].
researchlearning
06
Find holes in your argument or plan
Ask Claude to steel-man and then attack your idea. Finds weaknesses before your boss does.
▾
Here is my argument / plan / idea: [paste your idea] Do this in two parts: Part 1 — Steel-man it: make the strongest possible case FOR this idea, better than I did. Part 2 — Attack it: act as a sharp critic and find every weakness, assumption, gap, and risk. Be brutal. Don't protect my feelings. Then tell me: given both sides, what's the one thing I should fix first?
critical thinkingplanning
07
Compare two options objectively
Stop going back and forth. Get a clear, unbiased breakdown to make the decision faster.
▾
I'm deciding between [option A] and [option B]. My situation: [brief context about what you're trying to achieve] My constraints: [budget / time / skills / other limits] What matters most to me: [your top 2-3 priorities] Give me: 1. A side-by-side comparison on the criteria that actually matter for my situation 2. The hidden trade-offs most people miss 3. A clear recommendation with your reasoning 4. The one question I should ask myself before deciding
decision makingresearch
08
Turn any document into a study guide
Paste a textbook chapter, research paper, or report — get back a guide you can actually study from.
▾
Turn the following document into a proper study guide. Include: 1. A one-paragraph summary (the "so what") 2. Key concepts defined in plain English 3. 5 exam-style questions with answers 4. A "remember this" cheat sheet — the 5 most important points as bullets 5. One thing this document assumes I already know (that I might not) Here's the document: [paste your document, article, or chapter]

Write what goes ON the slide, not in the caption.

20
The self-review — make Claude improve its own output
The most powerful meta-prompt in this library. Run this after any Claude response to level it up.
▾
Look at the response you just gave me. Now do this: 1. Rate it honestly out of 10 — where did it fall short? 2. What did you assume that you shouldn't have? 3. What did I probably actually want that you didn't fully deliver? 4. Rewrite your response from scratch with those improvements. Be honest. A 6/10 that you improve is better than a 10/10 you pretend to give yourself.
